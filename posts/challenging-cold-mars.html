challenging the geologists' cold mars history
with bayesian diffusion models

Cameras tell us Mars was warm for hundreds of millions of years. Rocks tell us Mars was barely ever warm.

The cameras hint at existence of ancient water via erosion. Mars's atmosphere is known to have been thin for the last billion years since we see a consistent history of tiny meteorite craters (they would've burned up before impact if the atmosphere were thicker). So smooth pebbles and canyons are unmistakable signs of liquid water, but they're not strong bounds on how long Mars could've had liquid water. For example, the Grand Canyon on Earth only took 5ish million years to carve.

profile reconstruction
crater-age sequence

Martian craters give us one high lower bound around at least 100 million warm years (above 0°C). Imagine old and young meteorite craters. If there were one big ancient deluge, then no old craters would exist, and all young craters would have sharp lips. If there were one big recent deluge, then we wouldn't see craters at all. But if there were slow water erosion over eons, we'd see lots of craters with varying levels of lip smoothness. That's exactly what we see, with a total duration of at least 100My.

Using some clever forensics, geologists can repurpose silicates like feldspars into time-traveling thermometers. We have feldspars from Mars called Nakhlites, and a 2005 study by Shuster & Weiss (surprisingly) indicates that Mars could not have been warmer than 0°C for more than 10M years in the last billion.

Berkeley geochronologist Jack Carter and I suspected that the 21-year-old Shuster et al result is an overconfident point estimate (understandable since MCMC methods are essentially unusable without modern GPUs), so I pointed an 8xH100 cluster at reviving this problem with modern gradient-based MCMC samplers. I think that Mars could've been above freezing for 10x as long as we previously thought. Maybe not long enough for interesting astrobiology implications, but definitely long enough that canyons and smooth pebbles and craters lips are no longer confusing.

how to use a rock as a time-traveling thermometer

who pumped argon into martian rocks?

A feldspar is an aluminosilicate with a tiled tetrahedral backbone. Many feldspars contain potassium, but it's not bonded to anything; it kind of just sits in jail inside the crystal lattice. Since Potassium-40 (⁴⁰K) is radioactive, old feldspars will end up with some radiogenic Argon gas trapped inside.

when does the argon clock start?

Aluminosilicate magma has no crystal lattice to cage argon gas, so newly formed radiogenic ⁴⁰Ar escapes. The clock starts at closure: once cooling forms the feldspar lattice, newly produced argon can remain trapped.

how to date a feldspar

After closure, the remaining ⁴⁰K decays with a half-life of 1.25 billion years. About 10.7% of those decays produce radiogenic ⁴⁰Ar.

\[\begin{aligned} N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t)&=N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t_c)e^{-\lambda(t-t_c)}\\ N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{Ar}^{*}}(t)&=bN_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t_c)\left(1-e^{-\lambda(t-t_c)}\right), \qquad b=\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{Ar}}}{\lambda}\approx0.107 \end{aligned}\]

Measure the ⁴⁰K and radiogenic ⁴⁰Ar left in the feldspar, plug their ratio into this equation, and you get the time since closure.

\[t-t_c=\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln\!\left(1+\frac{1}{b}\frac{{}^{40}\mathrm{Ar}^{*}}{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}\right)\]

warmth siphons argon out of the rocks

Heat makes argon move faster through the crystal. Cool the rock down and diffusion nearly stops. The Arrhenius equation describes how violently the escape rate changes with temperature.

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t}&=\nabla\!\cdot\!\left(D(T)\nabla C\right)\\ D(T)&=D_0\exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a}{RT}\right) \end{aligned}\]

To measure this, geologists irradiate the sample so that ³⁹K produces ³⁹Ar, then put the feldspar in an oven and turn up the temperature step by step. The pattern of ³⁹Ar release tells us the diffusion kinetics. The amount of natural radiogenic ⁴⁰Ar that survived tells us which thermal histories the rock could have experienced.

There is no single closure temperature stamped on a feldspar. How much heat it can survive depends on the activation energy, the diffusion length, and how long it stays warm. That is what lets an argon age double as a thermometer.

augmenting shuster et al with modern computational bayesian models

shuster et al's 2-spike model

Real feldspars do not behave like one perfectly uniform argon jail. Multi-domain diffusion splits the sample into domains: the leaky domains release argon early, while the retentive domains hang onto it longer.

\[F_{\mathrm{model}}=\sum_i\phi_iF_i,\qquad\sum_i\phi_i=1\]

Shuster & Weiss's headline calculation split the sample into a low-retentivity domain and a high-retentivity domain that held most of the argon. If (x=\ln(D_0/a^2)) is the effective diffusion scale, that model looks like two spikes.

\[p(x)=\phi\,\delta(x-x_1)+(1-\phi)\,\delta(x-x_2)\]

Their supplement also compared one- and three-domain regressions and carried a model-form uncertainty envelope, so “two spikes” is a simplification of what they actually did. The modern question is narrower: how much uncertainty survives when we sample the diffusion parameters instead of settling on one fitted value?

These are effective diffusion lengths inferred from a release schedule, not microscope measurements of literal grain diameters.

the full posterior via NUTS

Instead of choosing one best set of diffusion parameters, I put a probability distribution over every plausible set. Bayes' rule scores each one by how well it reproduces the measured argon release.

\[p(\theta\mid y)\propto p(y\mid\theta)p(\theta)\]

A random-walk sampler stumbles blindly around this distribution. NUTS uses its slope to move through it much faster.

\[\log p(y\mid\theta)=-\frac12\sum_j\left[\frac{y_j-f_j(\theta)}{\sigma_j}\right]^2+\mathrm{const}.\]

I ran the model sweep with BlackJAX across eight H100s. Each comet-like streak is one chain searching through possible diffusion parameters. Every retained sample can be passed through the diffusion model to produce one possible temperature constraint.

The animation is a diagnostic ordered two-domain fit. It mixed cleanly—maximum (widehat R=1.003), more than 2,300 bulk effective samples, and no divergences—but it samples uncertainty inside the same two-domain structure. It does not demonstrate a continuous grain-size distribution.

was Mars warm?

For every posterior sample, I asked how hot Mars could stay before the rock lost more than 1% of its argon. Repeating that calculation across a trustworthy posterior would turn one temperature cutoff into a range. The timing of the warm interval matters too, because radiogenic argon can regrow afterward.

The old 1,000-chain run produced the exciting plot on the left: for a 100-million-year episode, a median cutoff of −12.7°C and a 95% interval from −21.1°C to 42.0°C. Its chains did not mix. Some (widehat R) values were above 10, so 42.0°C is not a defensible posterior endpoint.

The later model sweep has not recovered that warm-Mars result. The converged fixed-activation-energy, learned-noise fits keep their 95% intervals below 0°C; one of them also collapses its nominal three domains onto one effective diffusion scale. My current answer is that this analysis does not establish a 100-million-year thaw. It found a false positive, exposed several modeling choices that matter, and left a cleaner problem to solve.
