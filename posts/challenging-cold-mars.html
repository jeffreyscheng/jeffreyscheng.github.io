challenging the geologists' cold mars history with bayesian diffusion models

Rocks tell us one story and cameras tell us another. Orbital and rover images preserve channels, canyons, and rounded pebbles that are difficult to explain without flowing liquid water. Argon retained in Martian meteorites has been interpreted as evidence that the surface remained cold.

Shuster and Weiss used argon thermochronology to argue that the nakhlites had not spent more than about ten million years above 0°C during the last billion years. The analysis below asks how much of that result comes from the meteorite data and how much comes from the assumed diffusion-domain model.

The working hypothesis is that the rock record is informative, but that conditioning on two fixed diffusion domains made its thermal history appear more precise than the experiment supports.

Potassium-40 is radioactive. A population of potassium atoms decreases exponentially, while one branch of the decay produces argon-40. If the daughter argon remains in the mineral, the parent-to-daughter ratio records elapsed time.

N⁴⁰K(t) = N⁴⁰K(0)e−λt

A mineral age is therefore not only a clock reading. It also depends on whether the mineral remained closed to argon after crystallization.

Argon moves through feldspar by diffusion. The rate is strongly temperature-dependent: heating increases the diffusion coefficient and allows argon to leave the crystal; cooling suppresses diffusion and preserves it.

∂C/∂t = ∇ · (D(T)∇C) D(T) = D₀e−Eₐ/RT

Closure temperature is useful shorthand for this transition, but it is not a fixed material constant. It depends on the diffusion parameters, the size of the domain, and the cooling rate.

A feldspar grain need not behave as one diffusion domain. The multi-domain diffusion model represents the measured sample as a mixture of domains with different effective diffusion lengths. Small or fast domains release argon earlier; large or retentive domains release it later.

Fmodel = Σi φiFi,   Σi φi = 1

The inferred quantities are effective diffusion parameters and volume fractions. They should not automatically be read as a literal inventory of physical grain diameters.

In a step-heating experiment, the laboratory raises the temperature in stages and measures the argon released at each stage. A proposed parameter set is passed through the diffusion model to predict the release schedule.

log p(y | θ) = −½ Σj [(yj − fj(θ))/σj]2 + const.

The comparison between predicted and measured release supplies the likelihood. Thermal-history constraints are downstream of this fit, so uncertainty in the diffusion model must be carried through to the temperature calculation.

Shuster and Weiss represented the domain distribution with two components: a low-retentivity domain and a high-retentivity domain. In distribution notation, this is a pair of point masses.

p(x) = φδ(x − x₁) + (1 − φ)δ(x − x₂)

This was computationally practical in 2005. It also restricts the ways the model can explain the release data. If the true effective-domain distribution is broader or differently shaped, a two-point approximation can understate uncertainty in the most retentive material.

Bayesian inference treats the diffusion parameters as uncertain. The posterior combines the likelihood from the step-heating experiment with prior information about plausible parameter values.

p(θ | y) ∝ p(y | θ)p(θ)

MCMC constructs samples from this posterior. I used BlackJAX NUTS on eight H100 GPUs, with many chains exploring activation energies, effective diffusion lengths, and volume fractions. Each retained sample implies a possible domain model and, in turn, a possible temperature constraint.

For every posterior sample, I computed the highest constant temperature that would produce no more than 1% argon loss over several possible warm-episode durations. The collection of those temperatures gives a posterior interval rather than a single threshold.

For a 100-million-year episode, the median is −12.7°C and the 95% interval is [−21.1, 42.0]°C in the tracked 1,000-chain run. The upper interval crosses 0°C. This calculation does not show that Mars remained warm; it shows that this posterior does not rule out a warm episode as sharply as the two-domain point interpretation did.
