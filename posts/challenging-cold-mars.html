challenging the geologists' cold mars history
with bayesian diffusion models

Cameras tell us Mars was warm for hundreds of millions of years. Rocks tell us Mars was barely ever warm.

The cameras hint at existence of ancient water via erosion. Mars's atmosphere is known to have been thin for the last billion years since we see a consistent history of tiny meteorite craters (they would've burned up before impact if the atmosphere were thicker). So smooth pebbles and canyons are unmistakable signs of liquid water, but they're not strong bounds on how long Mars could've had liquid water. For example, the Grand Canyon on Earth only took 5ish million years to carve.

profile reconstruction
crater-age sequence

Martian craters give us one high lower bound around at least 100 million warm years (above 0°C). Imagine old and young meteorite craters. If there were one big ancient deluge, then no old craters would exist, and all young craters would have sharp lips. If there were one big recent deluge, then we wouldn't see craters at all. But if there were slow water erosion over eons, we'd see lots of craters with varying levels of lip smoothness. That's exactly what we see, with a total duration of at least 100My.

Using some clever forensics, geologists can repurpose silicates like feldspars into time-traveling thermometers. We have feldspars from Mars called Nakhlites, and a 2005 study by Shuster & Weiss (surprisingly) indicates that Mars could not have been warmer than 0°C for more than 10M years in the last billion.

Berkeley geochronologist Jack Carter and I suspected that the 21-year-old Shuster et al result was overconfident, so I revisited the inverse problem with a flexible diffusion model and gradient-based MCMC on eight GPUs. The result is narrower than I expected: Nakhla does not support a recent 100-million-year interval above freezing. An interval that old and beginning near the start of the last billion years reaches slightly above 0°C at the edge of its 95% posterior interval.

how to use a rock as a time-traveling thermometer

who pumped argon into martian rocks?

A feldspar is an aluminosilicate with a tiled tetrahedral backbone. Many feldspars contain potassium, but it's not bonded to anything; it kind of just sits in jail inside the crystal lattice. Since Potassium-40 (⁴⁰K) is radioactive, old feldspars will end up with some radiogenic Argon gas trapped inside.

when does the argon clock start?

Aluminosilicate magma has no crystal lattice to cage argon gas, so newly formed radiogenic ⁴⁰Ar escapes. The clock starts at closure: once cooling forms the feldspar lattice, newly produced argon can remain trapped. I call that moment \(t_{\mathrm{closure}}\).

how to date a feldspar

Let \(\Delta t=t-t_{\mathrm{closure}}\) be the elapsed time since closure, and let \(N_X(t)\) mean the number of atoms of isotope \(X\) at time \(t\). Potassium-40 has total decay constant \(\lambda=\ln(2)/(1.25\,\mathrm{Gyr})\). Only a fraction \(b\approx0.107\) of its decays take the argon-producing branch.

The star in \({}^{40}\mathrm{Ar}^{*}\) means radiogenic: argon produced by potassium decay, rather than all argon in the rock. In a closed feldspar, the remaining parent and accumulated daughter are

\[N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t) =N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t_{\mathrm{closure}}) e^{-\lambda\Delta t}\]
\[N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{Ar}^{*}}(t) =bN_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t_{\mathrm{closure}}) \left(1-e^{-\lambda\Delta t}\right)\]

Dividing daughter atoms by the remaining parent atoms gives a measured ratio \(R\):

\[R(t)\equiv \frac{N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{Ar}^{*}}(t)} {N_{{}^{40}\mathrm{K}}(t)} =b\left(e^{\lambda\Delta t}-1\right)\]

Solving that ratio for elapsed time gives the feldspar's time since closure:

\[\Delta t=\frac{1}{\lambda} \ln\!\left(1+\frac{R}{b}\right)\]

warmth siphons argon out of the rocks

Heat makes argon move faster through the crystal. Cool the rock down and diffusion nearly stops. The Arrhenius equation describes how violently the escape rate changes with temperature.

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t}&=\nabla\!\cdot\!\left(D(T)\nabla C\right)\\ D(T)&=D_0\exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a}{RT}\right) \end{aligned}\]

To measure this, geologists irradiate the sample so that ³⁹K produces ³⁹Ar, then put the feldspar in an oven and turn up the temperature step by step. The pattern of ³⁹Ar release tells us the diffusion kinetics. The amount of natural radiogenic ⁴⁰Ar that survived tells us which thermal histories the rock could have experienced.

There is no single closure temperature stamped on a feldspar. How much heat it can survive depends on the activation energy, the diffusion length, and how long it stays warm. That is what lets an argon age double as a thermometer.

augmenting shuster et al with modern computational bayesian models

shuster et al's 2-spike model

Shuster & Weiss reconstructed Nakhla's diffusion kinetics from a laboratory heating schedule. At each oven temperature, they measured the fraction of ³⁹Ar released from the rock. A multi-domain diffusion model treats the sample as a mixture of regions that release argon at different rates.

\[F_{\mathrm{model}}=\sum_i\phi_iF_i,\qquad\sum_i\phi_i=1\]

Their two-domain fit assigned 97% of the modeled volume to a high-retentivity domain (HRD) and 3% to a low-retentivity domain (LRD). If \(x=\ln(D_0/r^2)\) is the effective diffusion scale, the model is two point masses:

\[p(x)=\phi\,\delta(x-x_1)+(1-\phi)\,\delta(x-x_2)\]

Two domains are a useful summary of the release curve. For a temperature limit, the exact location and weight of the most retentive material matter enormously. I wanted to carry uncertainty in the whole diffusion-scale distribution through the thermal calculation.

the full posterior via NUTS

I replaced the two point masses with a smooth probability vector over 28 diffusion scales. The laboratory ³⁹Ar release constrains that distribution. The natural radiogenic-argon spectrum constrains temperature under a specified excursion duration and start time.

\[p(\theta,T\mid y_{39},y_{40},s)\propto p(y_{39}\mid\theta)\,p(y_{40}\mid\theta,T,s)\,p(\theta)\,p(T)\]

Here \(s\) is one duration-and-start-time cell. The sampler marginalizes over 21 mutually exclusive cells in one expanded ensemble. It sums their likelihoods; multiplying them would count the same rock spectrum repeatedly. Each draw carries a responsibility for every cell, which recovers the posterior conditional on that cell.

I ran 64 NUTS chains on eight A100 GPUs, with 2,000 adaptation steps and 4,000 retained draws per chain. The maximum rank-normalized and folded \(\widehat R\) was 1.0010. The minimum bulk and tail effective sample sizes were 83,413 and 96,173. There were no divergences or maximum-depth transitions. Every cell had at least 63,341 responsibility-weighted samples.

The animation predates the joint analysis. I could not recycle those samples: the earlier fit normalized by all extracted ³⁹Ar and used a legacy spherical-release approximation. The current fit uses the retained total through 850°C and exact spherical eigenmodes.

Importance reweighting confirms that the old sample does not cover the corrected target: Pareto \(k=3.44\), effective sample size 1.06 out of 25,600, and one draw receives 97.2% of the weight. A fresh posterior sample was necessary.

I ran a corrected laboratory-only control to separate the change in diffusion physics from the addition of the natural argon spectrum. Its maximum \(\widehat R\) was 1.0005, with zero divergences.

The old and corrected targets disagree, as the failed reweighting predicts. The corrected laboratory control and joint posterior agree closely: across six shared summaries and all 21 timing cells, their marginal-density overlap is 93.2–96.0%. The natural spectrum mostly identifies excursion temperature; it does not materially relearn the diffusion distribution.

The release schedule does not identify two exact domain sizes. It permits a broad peak near the high-retentivity domain and a small tail near the low-retentivity domain. The pale region is the pointwise central 95% interval after the natural spectrum and a 100-million-year recent excursion are included.

was Mars warm?

These are direct temperature posteriors from the joint likelihood. There is no 1%-argon-loss threshold. I used a uniform temperature prior from −100 to 100°C.

For a 100-million-year excursion ending now, the central 95% interval and median are [−94.45, −18.57, −4.47]°C. Only 0.25% of its conditional posterior is above freezing.

The long cold tails are not measurements of a −95°C climate. Below the temperature where diffusion becomes negligible, the rock cannot distinguish one cold temperature from another, so the lower tail follows the prior bound. The upper tail contains the useful constraint.

This Nakhla analysis covers the last 1.3 billion years. It does not test Noachian crater erosion several billion years ago, and it does not replace the ALH84001 half of Shuster & Weiss's four-billion-year argument. My original comparison put those time windows too close together.

The result does not support a recent 100-million-year interval above freezing. A 100-million-year interval near the beginning of the last billion years is not excluded at the central-95% level, but it has little posterior mass above 0°C under this prior.
